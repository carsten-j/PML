<section style="font-size:85%">
<h3>Sampling from a Normal distribution</h3>

<p>The Gaussian has no closed-form CDF or inverse CDF. </p>

<p>Both can be written in terms of the <em>error function</em>, \[\mathrm{erf}(x)=\frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_0^xe^{-t^2} dt\]</p>

\[\mathrm{cdf}(x) = \frac{1}{2}\left [1 + \mathrm{erf}\left( \frac{x-u}{\sigma\sqrt{2}}\right) \right] \hspace{2em} \mathrm{cdf}^{-1}(p) = \mu + \sigma \sqrt{2}~\mathrm{erf}^{-1}(2p - 1)\]

<p>The inverse CDF can, however, be <a href="https://www.jstor.org/stable/2347330">approximated accurately</a>, and the inverse transformation sampling approach can thus be implemented efficiently. </p> <!-- This is currently <a href="https://stat.ethz.ch/R-manual/R-devel/library/base/html/Random.html">the default in R</a>. </p> -->

<p class="fragment">However, we will use a slightly different approach here.</p>

</section>

<section style="font-size:70%">
<h3>Sampling from a Normal distribution - Box-Muller</h3>

<div class="container">
<div style="flex-grow: 3.5;">
<p>Another common approach is to use the Box-Muller transform. Consider a joint distribution of two independent standard Gaussians</p>

\[p(x_1, x_2) = p(x_1)p(x_2) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-\frac{(x_1^2+x_2^2)}{2}}\]
<p class="fragment" data-fragment-index="2">The density is rotationally symmetric, so we'll write it in terms of an angular component \(\theta=\tan^{-1}(\frac{x_2}{x_1})\) and a squared radial component \(s=(x_1^2+x_2^2)\)</p>
</div>
<div>
<img class="fragment" data-fragment-index="2" style="margin:1em;" src="gaussian_angle_radial.svg">
</div>
</div>

<div class="fragment" class="fragment" data-fragment-index="3" style="float:right; width:40%; border:1px solid lightgrey; padding:0.5em; font-size:85%">
\(x_1 = \sqrt{s} \cos(\theta)\hspace{2em}\) \(x_2 = \sqrt{s} \sin(\theta)\)<br>
\(\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial s} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{s}}\cos(\theta)\hspace{2em}\)
\(\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} = -\sqrt{s}\sin(\theta)\) <br>
\(\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial s} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{s}}\sin(\theta)\hspace{2em}\)
\(\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial \theta} = \sqrt{s}\cos(\theta)\)
</div>

<div class="container">
<div style="flex-grow: 1.5" class="fragment" class="fragment" data-fragment-index="3">
<p>Changing variables, \[p(s, \theta) = p_{x_1, x_2}(x_1(s,\theta), x_2(s, \theta)){\underbrace{\begin{vmatrix}\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial s}&\frac{\partial x_1}{\partial \theta} \\[0.5em] \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial s}&\frac{\partial x_2}{\partial \theta}\end{vmatrix}}_{\tiny \text{Jacobian determinant}}}\] </p>

</div>
</div>

<span class="fragment" data-fragment-index="3">


<!-- \[p(d, \theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-\frac{d}{2}}{\underbrace{\begin{vmatrix}2x_1 & 2x_2 \\ \frac{1}{1+(\frac{x_2}{x_1})^2} \frac{-x_2}{x_1^2} & \frac{1}{x_1(1+(\frac{x_2}{x_1})^2)}\end{vmatrix}}_{\tiny \text{Jacobian determinant} \begin{vmatrix}\frac{\partial d}{\partial x_1}&\frac{\partial d}{\partial x_2} \\[0.5em] \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x_1}&\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x_2}\end{vmatrix}}}^{-1} = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-\frac{d}{2}}\begin{vmatrix}2x_1 & 2x_2 \\ \frac{-x_2}{x_1^2+x_2^2} & \frac{x_1}{x_1^2 + x_2^2}\end{vmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \frac{1}{2}e^{-\frac{d}{2}} \]-->

</span>

The angle and squared radial components are thus independent, and we know how to sample for each of these.</p>

</section>

<section style="font-size:75%">
<h3>Sampling from a Normal distribution - Box-Muller (2)</h3>

<p>Summary of idea: Sample an angle from a uniform distribution and a squared distance from an exponential distribution. The x and y coordinates of this sample will be independently normally distributed.</p>

Procedure:<br>
<ol>
<li>Sample a pair of values, \(u_1, u_2\), from \(\mathcal{U}(0,1)\).</li>
<li>Use inverse transform sample technique to generate samples for \(s\) and \(\theta\)

<!-- \[d = -2\ln(1-u_1) \hspace{3em} \theta = 2\pi u_2\]-->
\[\begin{align*}
s &= -2\ln(1-u_1) \hspace*{1em} \text{(or: } -2\ln(u_1) \text{, since }(1-u_1)\text{ is also }\mathcal{U}(0,1)\\
\theta &= 2\pi u_2 \\
\end{align*}\]
</li>
<li>Generate \(x_1\) and \(x_2\)

\[\begin{align*}
x_1 &= \sqrt{s} \cos(\theta) \\
x_2 &= \sqrt{s} \sin(\theta)
\end{align*}\]
</li>
</ol>

<p>There are variants of the Box-Muller that avoid the trigonometric functions.</p>

<dl class="list">
<dt>Wrap the target distribution \(\tilde p(z)\) in an "envelope" distribution \(q(z)\) which we can draw samples from.</dt>
</dl>

</div>
<div>

<dt class="fragment">Draw pairs of samples: \(z \sim q(z)\) and \(u \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)\)</dt>

</dl>

</dl>

</section>

<section style="font-size:90%">
<h3>Rejection sampling - why does it work?</h3>

<div class="container">
<div style="flex-grow: 2">
<p>Consider the joint distribution of \(z\) and a boolean variable \(a\) indicating acceptance of the sample.

</div>
<div>
<img class="noborder center" src="../prmlfigs-svg/Figure11.4.svg" alt="">
</div>
</div>

The marginal of \(p(z)\) is given by the envelope \(q(z)\) (this is what we sample from).

The probability density of \(z\) <em>given acceptance</em> is:</p>
\[p(z|a) = \frac{p(a|z)q(z)}{p(a)} = \frac{p(a|z)q(z)}{\int_z p(a|z)q(z)} \]

<span class="fragment">
<p>By construction, \(p(a|z)\) is \(\frac{\tilde p(z)}{kq(z)}\), so we have</p>

\[p(z|a) = \frac{\frac{1}{k}\tilde p(z)}{\frac{1}{k}\underbrace{\int_z\tilde p(z)}_{Z_p}} = \frac{1}{Z_p}\tilde p(z) = p(z)\]
</span>
</section>

<section>
<h3>Rejection sampling - How efficient is it?</h3>

<div class="container">
<div style="flex-grow: 2">
<p>From the denominator in the previous slide, we can read off the marginal probability of acceptance:</p>

</div>
<div>
<img class="noborder center" src="../prmlfigs-svg/Figure11.4.svg" alt="">
</div>
</div>

\[p(a) = \int_z p(a|z)q(z) = \int_z\frac{\tilde p(z)}{kq(z)}q(z) = \frac{Z_p}{k}\]

<p class="fragment">The area under the envelope is \(k\), so this is a ratio between the area under \(\tilde p(z)\) and the area under \(kq(z)\).</p>

<p class="fragment">Long story short: we achieve the best performance when the grey region in the figure is as small as possible. I.e., we want a "tight" envelope.</p>

</section>

<section style="text-align:center">
<h2>Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)</h2>
</section>

<section>
<p>Often, it is difficult to come up with a good enough proposal distribution for importance or rejection sampling - in particular in high dimensions</p>

<p>Markov Chain Monte Carlo is a framework for overcoming these problems, making it possible to sample fruitfully from a very broad range of distributions.</p>

<p class="fragment">Idea: we generate <em><strong>a series of samples, where each sample depends on the previous one</strong></em>. We will see that this makes it easier to come up with a meaningful proposal distribution.</p>
</section>

<section>
<h3>What is a Markov Chain?</h3>

<p>Markov chain: series of random variables \( z^{(1)}, \ldots, z^{(L)}\) which factorizes like:</p>

\[p(z^{(1)}, \ldots, z^{(L)}) = p(z^{(1)})p(z^{(2)}|z^{(1)}), \ldots, p(z^{(L)}| z^{(L-1)})\]

<img class="noborder center" style="width:60%; margin:1em auto" src="prml_figure13.3_adapted.svg">

<p class="fragment">We will often work with <em>homogeneous</em> Markov models, where \(p(z^{(l)}|z^{(l-1)})\) is identical for all \(l\).</p>

<p class="fragment">For convenience, we will develop the concepts in terms of discrete Markov Chains.</p>

<p class="fragment">In this case, the \(p(z^{(l)}|z^{(l-1)})\) conditional probabilities are referred to as <em>transition probabilities</em>.
<!-- \(T_l(z^{(l-1)}, z^{(l)})\)</p>-->
</section>

<section style="font-size:90%">
<h3>Markov Chains: stationary distribution</h3>

The marginal distribution for any of the variables in the chain can be written as:
\[p(z^{(l)}) = \sum_{z^{(l-1)}}p(z^{(l)} | z^{(l-1)})p(z^{(l-1)})\]

<span class="fragment">
<p>If a marginal distribution \(p(z^{(l)})\) is invariant wrt the transition probabilities, then it is referred to as a <em>stationary distribution</em> of the Markov Chain. </p>

<p>For a homogeneous Markov Chain, for a transition \(z' \rightarrow z\) we can write this as:</p>

\[p^*(z) = \sum_{z'}p(z|z')p^*(z')\]
</span>

<span class="fragment">After letting the chain for a long time, this is the distribution over the states of z that we will converge to.</span>
</section>

<section style="font-size:80%">
<h3>Markov Chains: detailed balance</h3>

<p>We have: \(p^*(z)\) is a stationary distribution of a Markov chain if:</p>

\[p^*(z) = \sum_{z'}p^*(z')p(z|z') \hspace{5em}(1)\]

<p>Goal: we want to <em>design</em> the Markov chain to have a <em>specific</em> stationary distribution. How can we do that?</p>

<span class="fragment">
<p>Solution: By constructing the transition probabilities to obey <em>detailed balance</em>:</p>

\[p^*(z')p(z|z') = p^*(z)p(z'|z) \hspace{3em} \text{\tiny (Sufficient condition - although not necessary)}\]
</span>

<span class="fragment">
<p>We can convince ourselves that this is true by substituting in eq (1):</p>

<!-- \[\sum_{z'}\underbrace{p^*(z)p(z' | z)}_{rhs} = p^*(z)\underbrace{\sum_{z'}p(z' | z)}_{\begin{gather*}\text{\tiny normalized density} \\[-.5em] \text{\tiny sums to 1}\end{gather*}} = p^*(z) \hspace{2em} = \sum_{z'}\underbrace{p^*(z')p(z | z')}_{lhs}\]-->
\[\sum_{z'}\underbrace{p^*(z')p(z | z')}_{\text{lhs}} = \sum_{z'}\underbrace{p^*(z)p(z' | z)}_{\text{subst with rhs}} = p^*(z)\underbrace{\sum_{z'}p(z' | z)}_{\begin{gather*}\text{\tiny normalized distribution} \\[-.5em] \text{\tiny sums to 1}\end{gather*}} = p^*(z)\]
</span>

</section>

<section style="font-size:90%">
<h3>Markov Chains: ergodicity</h3>

<p>In addition to being invariant, we also want to make sure that the Markov Chain actually converges to our target distribution as we let the length of the chain approach infinity.</p>

<span class="fragment">
For this to be true, the chain should be:
<dl class="list">
<dt>Irreducible. There should be a sequence of non-zero probability transitions from any state \(z_s\) to any other state \(z_t\).</dt>
<dt class="fragment">Aperiodic. The probability of returning to the same state should not have a periodic dependence on the number of steps.</dt>
</dl>

<p class="fragment">If this is fulfilled, the chain is said to be <em>ergodic</em>.</p>
</span>
</section>


<section style="font-size:75%">
<h3>Metropolis-Hastings</h3>

<div class="container">
<div style="flex-grow:1; padding-right:1em; padding-top:0.0em">
<p>We need to come up with some \(p(z|z')\) that fulfills detailed balance:</p>
</div>
<div>
<div style="border:1px solid lightgrey; background-color: #fafafa; padding:1em; padding-top:0.5em; padding-bottom:0.5em; font-size:100%">
Detailed balance equation:
\[p(z')p(z|z') = p(z)p(z'|z) \]

</div>
</div>
</div>

<span class="fragment">
<p>Idea: factorize \(p(z|z')\) into:

\(p(z|z') = \underbrace{q(z|z')}_{\text{\tiny proposal}}\underbrace{\alpha(z, z')}_{\text{\tiny acceptance}}\)</p>

</span>

<span class="fragment">
<p>We can now choose \(q\) freely, and then choose \(\alpha\) carefully to ensure detailed balance:</p>

\[p(z')q(z|z')\alpha(z', z) = p(z)q(z'|z)\alpha(z, z') \hspace{1em}\Rightarrow \hspace{1em}\frac{\alpha(z', z)}{\alpha(z, z')} = \frac{p(z)q(z'|z)}{p(z')q(z|z')}\]
</span>

<div class="container fragment">
<div>
<p>One way to satisfy this equation is:</p>

\[\alpha(z, z') = \min\left( 1, \frac{p(z)q(z'|z)}{p(z')q(z|z')}\right)\]
</div>
<div style="display: flex; align-items: center;">
<p style="margin:0; margin-top:1em">This is called the <em>Metropolis-Hastings</em> algorithm.</p>
</div>
</div>

</section>

<section style="font-size:85%">
<h3>Metropolis-Hastings: choosing a proposal</h3>

<p>Choosing good proposals is a science in itself.</p>
<div class="container">
<div class="fragment" data-fragment-index="1" style="flex-grow: 2">
<p>Common strategy: use Gaussian centered around current state.</p>

<p class="fragment" data-fragment-index="2">The choice of variance should now reflect the smallest length scale:</p>
</div>
<div class="fragment" data-fragment-index="1">
<div class="r-stack" >

</div>
</div>
</div>

<span class="fragment">
<p>With symmetric proposals like this, the acceptance probability simplifies to</p>
\[\alpha(z, z') = \min\left( 1, \frac{p(z)}{p(z')}\right) \hspace*{3em}\text{this is the original "Metropolis" algorithm}\]
</span>
</section>

<section style="font-size:90%">
<h3>Metropolis-Hastings - conclusion</h3>

<p>Problem: We have a target density \(p(z)\) that we can evaluate, but not sample from.</p>
<dl class="list">
<dt class="fragment">We can do a random walk, according to a Markov Chain defined simply by a transition probability.</dt>
<dt class="fragment">If we accept/reject appropriately, this will lead to samples from \(p(z)\).</dt>
</dl>

<p class="fragment">Consider how amazing this is.</p>

<p class="fragment">Note, however - that we do not get <em>independent</em> samples from \(p(z)\). This is the price we pay. Often, we thus subsample from our samples in order to get (roughly) independent samples.</p>

</section>

<h3>Gibbs sampling</h3>

<p>Important special case: </p>
<p>Setup: You have \(M\) variables, and you wish to sample from \(p(\boldsymbol z) = p(z_1, z_2, \ldots z_M)\). As usual, we will assume that sampling from \(p(\boldsymbol z)\) is difficult. </p>

<p>However, let's imagine that it is possible to draw samples from \(p(z_i | z_{\setminus i})\), i.e. the distribution of each variable conditioned on the rest.</p>

<h3>Gibbs sampling in terms of Metropolis-Hastings</h3>

<p>We can show that Gibbs is a valid sampler by considering it in the Metropolis-Hastings framework.</p>

<p>We consider variable \(z_i\). Note that:</p>

<dl class="list">
<dt>\(z'_{\setminus i} = z_{\setminus i}\) (the other variables are fixed)</dt>
<dt>\(p(\boldsymbol z) = p(z_i | \boldsymbol z_{\setminus i})p(\boldsymbol z_{\setminus i})\)</dt>
</dl>

<!-- \(z'_{\setminus i} = z_{\setminus i}\) (the other variables are fixed)</p>-->

<p>Detailed balance:</p>
\[\begin{align*}\alpha(\boldsymbol z, \boldsymbol z') &= \min\left( 1, \frac{p(\boldsymbol z)q(\boldsymbol z'|\boldsymbol z)}{p(\boldsymbol z')q(\boldsymbol z|\boldsymbol z')}\right) \\
&= \min\left( 1, \frac{p(z_i | \boldsymbol z_{\setminus i})p(\boldsymbol z_{\setminus i})p(z'_i | \boldsymbol z_{\setminus i})}{p(z'_i | \boldsymbol z_{\setminus i})p(\boldsymbol z'_{\setminus i})p(z_i | \boldsymbol z_{\setminus i})}\right) = 1
\end{align*}\]

<p>We see that we indeed need no acceptance step in a Gibbs sample.</p>
